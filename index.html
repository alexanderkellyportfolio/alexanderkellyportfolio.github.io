SXSW BattleBot IQ

Hardware Engineer, Structural Design & Manufacturing

5 weeks · 15-lb class · 2nd place at SXSW

SolidWorksManual MillingLaser CuttingMaterial SelectionDFM/DFATolerance AnalysisImpact Load Analysis

Context

UT's robotics department put out the call for a team to build a 15-lb combat robot for SXSW with five weeks to go from nothing to competing. I signed up and pulled together a team. My role was the structural side: the backplate, wheel guards, modular forks, and the mounting scheme that tied it all together. From day one I designed everything around field repairability, because we knew parts were going to get hit and we needed to be able to swap them between rounds.

Full assembly, top view

Material Selection

The backplate needed to be strong, light, and machinable in our shop, so I compared three alloys. 7075-T6 is stronger at 570 MPa yield but it's brittle under impact loading, which is a serious concern when the whole robot is a target. 2024 is aerospace-grade overkill at our budget. 6061-T6 at 275 MPa yield and 2.7 g/cm3 was the right call: good strength-to-weight ratio and it machines cleanly on a manual mill.

For the wheel guards I tested three thicknesses of HDPE. 3mm cracked on impact. 8mm was too heavy. 6mm at Shore D 66 hardness hit the sweet spot: opponents slide off on contact and the material absorbs energy elastically without shattering. The weapon drum is 4340 hardened steel, heat treated to 470 MPa yield, since it needs to survive direct metal-on-metal hits repeatedly.

SolidWorks CAD model

We came in at 14.6 lb, 97% of the 15 lb limit. I held back 0.4 lb deliberately as repair margin, since we'd be swapping out damaged parts between matches and couldn't afford to go overweight mid-tournament.

Manufacturing

I milled the backplate on a manual Bridgeport at 1000 RPM with a 6mm 2-flute HSS end mill, 100 mm/min feed, 0.5mm depth of cut. I chose HSS over carbide because carbide tends to chip on interrupted cuts, and a structural plate with holes and pockets is basically all interrupted cuts. Surface finish came out around Ra 3.2 microns, which is fine for a structural part.

Milling the backplate, 1000 RPM, 100mm/min feed

The HDPE wheel guards were laser cut on a Trotec Speedy 300 at 80W with a slow 10 mm/s feed. Any faster and the material melts rather than cuts cleanly.

Engineering Details

The drum spins up to about 3000 RPM through a belt drive. I derived the moment of inertia from the CAD geometry since the irregular drum shape makes hand calculations unreliable. From high-speed video of test hits, metal-on-metal contact lasts about 10ms, and roughly half the stored energy transfers per hit.

Stored Kinetic Energyw = 3000 x 2pi/60 = 314 rad/s
KE = 0.5 x I x w^2 = 0.5 x 0.008 x 314^2
KE = 395 J

From high-speed video, metal-on-metal contact is about 10ms. Roughly half the energy transfers per hit.

Peak Impact ForceF = 200 J / 0.01 s
F = 20 kN peak

Design target: 3x FOS = 60 kN structural capacity

Bearing press-fits are H7/h6, light interference of 0.01 to 0.02mm. That's tight enough that the bearings don't walk under vibration but you can still press them out if you need to swap one between rounds. The weapon shaft hex hub sees 87 MPa bearing stress on 4140 steel at 415 MPa yield, giving a FOS of 4.8. Wheel guard mounting uses a clearance fit (H8/f7) so guards can be pulled off and replaced quickly without tools.

I needed to confirm the backplate wouldn't yield during a head-on hit. I estimated peak impact force from the weapon energy calculation at 20 kN worst case, then modeled the backplate as a plate rather than a simple beam since the width-to-span ratio makes beam theory inaccurate here.

Backplate Bending6061-T6, 200mm span, 150mm wide, 6mm thick
I = (150 x 6^3)/12 = 2700 mm^4
M_max = FL/4 = 20000 x 0.2/4 = 1000 N m
sigma = M*c/I = 1000*0.003/2.7e-9 = 1111 kPa...

Actually modeled as plate, not beam:
sigma = (6*M)/(b*t^2) = (6*1000)/(0.15*0.006^2)
sigma = 111 MPa vs 275 MPa yield, FOS = 2.5

Eight M4 grade 12.9 socket heads mount the backplate to the frame. During a side impact they see combined shear and tension. I checked both bolt shear capacity and aluminum tearout. Tearout came out at FOS 6.3, so the bolts are the limiting case at FOS 2.6, which is appropriate for dynamic impact loading. All fasteners got Loctite 243 to prevent vibration loosening during the matches.

Grade 12.9 shear strength: 0.6 x 1220 = 732 MPa
Capacity per bolt: 732 x 8.78 = 6.4 kN
FOS = 6.4/2.5 = 2.6 per bolt

One fork took a bad hit and I had it swapped out in about ten minutes between rounds. The backplate, weapon shaft, and all bearings made it through the full event without any failures.

RC Car Drivetrain

Design & Fabrication Engineer, Drivetrain

8-week academic project

OnshapeANSYS FEADrivetrain DesignFailure AnalysisBelt DrivesShaft SizingBearing SelectionTolerance Analysis

Context

This was a class project to build a full RC car from scratch. I owned the rear-wheel-drive drivetrain and all the associated mounting hardware. The front end used an off-the-shelf Ackermann steering kit. My first drivetrain design failed in testing, and the redesign process ended up being more educational than the original design work.

Initial design with spur gears

Gear Failure and Redesign

The first drivetrain used 3D-printed PLA spur gears in two stages (10T to 20T, then 15T to 48T) for a 6.4:1 total reduction. After about 15 to 20 minutes of running, the teeth stripped. The root cause was backlash in the gear mesh: every time the motor transitioned between driving and coasting, the slack caused an impact load on the teeth. PLA fatigue strength is around 30 MPa at a million cycles, and the dynamic loading was pushing tooth stress to about 50 MPa. The math said it was going to fail, and it did.

Redesigned with timing belt system

I redesigned around HTD timing belts with 5mm pitch. Same 6.4:1 ratio across two stages (18T to 36T, then 25T to 80T), so the vehicle dynamics stayed the same. The real gain was zero backlash from positive tooth engagement, plus the belt absorbs transient loads elastically, cutting peak stress by about 40%. It ran 40 plus minutes without issues.

HTD timing belt and pulley assembly

Engineering Details

After the gear failure I wasn't going to assume the new pulley mount was fine without checking it. I ran ANSYS with 50N belt tension at peak acceleration load and PLA material properties. Max von Mises stress came out at 12 MPa at the bolt hole, giving a FOS of 4.2 against PLA's 50 MPa yield.

The front end uses an Ackermann steering kit. Ackermann geometry ensures the inside wheel turns more than the outside through a corner, so both wheels track their own arc instead of scrubbing. For our wheelbase and track width, the inside wheel needs to turn 9.7 degrees more than the outside at minimum radius.

Outer = arctan(200/450) = 24.0 deg

The intermediate shaft sees both torsion from torque transfer and bending from belt tension. At 4mm radius in 1045 steel, combined von Mises stress comes out to 80 MPa against a 310 MPa yield, giving a FOS of 3.9. Bearing life is essentially unlimited at this load level. The real failure mode is going to be dust contamination, not fatigue.

Slider-Crank Mechanism

ME 350R, Mechanism Design & Fabrication

Laser cutting · 3D printing · Press-fit iteration

Laser Cutting3D Printing (PETG)Mechanism DesignGrashof AnalysisBeam BendingPress-Fit DesignTolerance Iteration

Overview

The assignment was to build a working Grashof four-bar mechanism: less than 2mm of joint play, less than 3mm out-of-plane motion, and able to support a 10N load over a 50mm stroke. I laser-cut the linkages from acrylic on the Trotec Speedy 300 and printed the bearing holders, standoffs, and feet in PETG on a Bambu P1P. Getting the press-fit bearings dialed in took more iteration than expected.

CAD model with link dimensions

Press-Fit Iteration

This was the hardest part of the project. The rotary bearings are 6mm ID and getting a tight press-fit in laser-cut acrylic without cracking it requires some iteration. My first test cut used 6.0mm holes, which left about 0.3mm of play. The bearing just rattled in there. Second attempt at 5.9mm brought it down to 0.1mm, which was better but still not where I wanted it. Third round at 5.85mm gave less than 0.05mm play. Assembly needed a light tap with a mallet (maybe 50N of force) but the fit was solid and nothing cracked.

Press-fit test cuts: 6.0mm, 5.9mm, 5.85mm

Laser cut test pieces, dialing in kerf compensation

Engineering Details

Before cutting anything I verified the link lengths satisfy the Grashof condition, which guarantees the crank can rotate a full 360 degrees rather than just rocking back and forth. The condition requires that the sum of the shortest and longest links be less than or equal to the sum of the other two.

Link LengthsL1 = 100mm (ground), L2 = 60mm (crank)
L3 = 120mm (coupler), L4 = 80mm (slider)

s + l ≤ p + q
60 + 120 = 180 ≤ 100 + 80 = 180
Grashof satisfied, crank rotates 360°

The 10N load spec means the longest unsupported link, a 100mm acrylic cantilever, needs to carry that load without snapping or deflecting enough to bind the mechanism. I ran bending stress and deflection calculations before cutting to confirm 6mm thick acrylic would hold.

Bending StressM = 10N x 0.1m = 1 N m
S = (20 x 6^2)/6 = 120 mm^3
sigma = 1000/120 = 8.3 MPa
FOS = 70/8.3 = 8.4
Deflectiondelta = FL^3/(3EI) = (10 x 100^3)/(3 x 3000 x 360)
delta = 0.31mm, well within tolerance

Results

Final assembled mechanism

Hit all four specs. The tightest margin was out-of-plane motion at 2.8mm against a 3mm limit, which came down to how square everything assembled and how accurately the bearing holders printed. Joint play came in at 1.9mm, just under the 2mm limit, which is where all the press-fit iteration paid off.

Arduino Robot Car

ME 350R, Mechatronics & Electronics Integration

Differential drive · All-soldered connections

Arduino (C/C++)SolderingLaser Cutting3D Printing (PETG)Circuit DesignPWM Motor ControlPower Budgeting

Overview

The assignment was a joystick-controlled robot capable of carrying a 1kg payload, with cable management graded as a separate criterion. Rather than wiring everything up and cleaning it later, I planned the routing from the start. I used custom-length wires cut and soldered to exact lengths with heat shrink on every connection, zip ties at planned anchor points, and velcro slots in the chassis for the battery. No breadboards anywhere.

Top view, all wires within 2mm clearance

Electronics & Controls

An Arduino Uno runs two 6V DC gearmotors (48:1 gearbox, 200 RPM no-load) through an L298N dual H-bridge. The joystick outputs 0 to 1023 on the analog pins. I added a deadzone of plus or minus 50 counts around center to stop the motors from drifting when the joystick is at rest. Without it, analog noise causes the motors to creep. For steering I mixed X and Y axes: left motor gets Y plus half of X, right motor gets Y minus half of X, both clamped to the PWM range. It gives smooth differential steering with no jerky transitions.

CAD layout with routing channels and mount locations

Power DrawMotors at 50% load: 2 × 0.5A × 6V = 6W
Arduino: 50mA × 5V = 0.25W
L298N losses (~80% eff): ~1.5W
Total: 7.75W → 1.55 hrs on 4×AA NiMH

Mechanical Design

The chassis is a 150 by 200mm laser-cut acrylic base with both the bottom and top plate at 6mm thick. Motor mounts are PETG printed to fit the 37mm gearbox diameter with a 4-point bolt pattern. The caster wheel mount has 5mm of height adjustment for leveling. For the structural test I loaded 1kg on the top plate and measured deflection at center with a dial indicator.

CNC Ball in Cube

CNC Machining & CAM Programming

3 weeks · 6 setups · ~4 hours machine time

Fusion360 CAM3-Axis CNCMulti-Setup WorkholdingToolpath OptimizationFeeds & SpeedsSurface FinishGD&T

Overview

The challenge was to machine a free-floating ball trapped inside a cube, starting from a solid 3-inch block of 6061-T6 aluminum. The ball has to exist inside the cube from the moment it's freed: no assembly after the fact. That means cutting away material from all six faces in sequence, four operations per face, without releasing the ball until the very last pass on the very last face. Mess up the sequencing or lose workholding on face five and there is no recovery.

Fusion360 CAM, adaptive clearing toolpath

Process

Every face follows the same sequence: rough with a 6mm flat end mill, finish the sphere surface with a 6mm ball nose, clean up the edges, then add a 0.5mm by 45 degree chamfer. The real challenge is workholding. By face five you are clamping on finished surfaces, so I printed PETG jaw pads to protect them. For the final face I left a 0.5mm web intact until every other operation was complete, then broke through carefully by hand.

After roughing, Ra ~3.2μm

5 of 6 faces done

Roughing runs at 8000 RPM, 800 mm/min feed, 1.5mm stepover, 3mm depth of cut. Finishing drops to 10,000 RPM, 600 mm/min, 0.2mm stepover. I verified the cutting forces were within the Haas VF-2's capability by calculating the specific cutting force for 6061-T6 and checking spindle torque utilization before running the program.

Cutting Force Checkkc (6061-T6) ≈ 600 N/mm²
Fc = 600 × 3 × 1.5 = 2700N
Spindle torque = 2700 × 0.003 = 8.1 N·m
Haas VF-2 max at 8000 RPM = 26.3 N·m
31% utilization, well within capability

Results

The ball came free on the final pass. I checked surface finish with a profilometer after hand sanding and measured cube flatness and sphere diameter with calipers and a dial indicator against the CAD nominal dimensions.

Custom Graduation Gift

Overview

Every year at Texas Inventionworks, the team builds a custom gift for outgoing seniors. I designed a two-material layered piece using clear acrylic and birch plywood, connected through press-fit tabs. The design constraint I set for myself was making the joints strong enough to hold together reliably without relying on adhesives, even though they were allowed.

Top view, 5 material layers

Press-Fit Design

Laser kerf varies between materials: 0.15mm for acrylic on the Trotec, 0.12mm for wood. I cut tabs at 10mm wide and slots at 9.7mm to leave about 0.15mm of interference after kerf compensation. Each joint takes around 20N to press together by hand. With 8 joints that gives 160N of total retention force against a roughly 50N requirement for normal handling, about a 3.2 factor of safety.

Red Cross Cooling Station

Thermal Analysis Lead, ME 266K Senior Design

Full semester · IFRC client · CFD & thermal analysis

SolidWorks Flow SimCFDHeat TransferHVAC SizingPsychrometricsTurbulence ModelingPower Budgeting

Problem

Our senior design client was the International Red Cross. They needed a cooling station that could be deployed quickly during extreme heat emergencies: 20 or more people sheltering in a tent, outdoor temperatures above 40 degrees C, running off a generator with no fixed infrastructure. My role on the team was thermal analysis lead. I calculated the total heat load, sized the AC system, and ran CFD to validate that the design would actually keep occupants below safe temperature thresholds before we committed to hardware.

Thermal Load Breakdown

I worked through every heat source independently rather than using a rule-of-thumb estimate, because the scenario is extreme enough that rounding errors matter. Solar radiation alone accounts for more than half the total load.

CFD Validation

I set up the simulation in SolidWorks Flow Sim with a 250,000-cell tetrahedral mesh, refined to 5cm near the AC vents and occupant zones where the flow behavior is most complex. Boundary conditions were 40 degrees C ambient with 2 m/s wind, AC supply at 15 degrees C and 400 CFM.

The hot-dry scenario (40 degrees C, 20% RH) came out to 24.5 degrees C average interior temperature, right on the 25 degree target. There is about 5 degrees of stratification from floor to ceiling, which is expected. The heat flux through the walls matched my hand calculations at 30 W/m2, which confirmed the simulation was set up correctly.

The humid scenario (35 degrees C, 80% RH) was more marginal at 26 degrees C average, slightly above the 25 degree target. The wet-bulb temperature came in at 23 degrees C though, which is still within safe limits for prolonged exposure per OSHA guidelines. Humidity adds a latent load that the dry scenario doesn't capture: 20 people sweating contributes about 628W of additional moisture load on top of the sensible heat.

The 38,200 BTU/hr total load requires a minimum of 3.2 tons. I sized to a 3-ton unit, about 6% undersized at absolute peak, which is acceptable because peak solar only lasts a few hours per day and occupants can tolerate brief excursions above 25 degrees C. I also evaluated evaporative cooling (300W draw, effective in dry conditions but useless above 80% RH), passive ventilation (would require over 100 degrees C interior temperature to move enough heat, which is obviously not viable), and a solar plus battery option ($9,500 versus $3,000 for diesel, better suited to permanent installations).
