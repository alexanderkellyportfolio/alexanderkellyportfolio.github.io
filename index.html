SXSW BattleBot IQ

Hardware Engineer — Structural Design & Manufacturing

5 weeks · 15-lb class · 2nd place finish

Full assembly — top view

SolidWorks CAD — full assembly

Weapon system detail

Milling the backplate — 1000 RPM, 100mm/min feed, 0.5mm DOC

Before competition

After 8 matches — 3 wheel guard replacements, 1 fork swap

The team at SXSW

Overview

Five weeks before SXSW, UT's robotics department needed a 15-lb combat robot. Absurdly quick timeline—nothing to competition in five weeks. I owned the structural design: backplate, wheel guards, modular forks, and the mounting scheme that held everything together. The whole design philosophy was built around field repairability—we knew stuff was going to break, so we made it easy to swap.

Material Selection

The backplate is 6061-T6 aluminum—275 MPa yield, 2.7 g/cm³ density, which gives a strength-to-weight ratio of about 102 MPa·cm³/g. Considered 7075-T6 (stronger at 570 MPa but brittle under impact and awful to weld) and 2024 (aerospace-grade overkill for our budget). 6061 was the right call: strong, machinable, and we could repair it if needed.

Wheel guards are HDPE—Shore D 66 hardness, low friction (μ ≈ 0.15-0.20). The beauty of HDPE is it absorbs impacts elastically without brittle fracture. Opponents literally slide off. We tested 3mm (cracked), 6mm (absorbed hits), and 8mm (too heavy). 6mm won. The weapon drum is 4340 hardened steel, heat treated to 470 MPa yield—hard enough to retain its cutting edge through multiple impacts.

Engineering Deep Dive

The drum weapon stores serious energy. We measured the moment of inertia on a torsion pendulum (I ≈ 0.008 kg·m²) and the belt system limits us to about 3000 RPM.

Stored Kinetic Energy ω = 3000 × 2π/60 = 314 rad/s
KE = ½Iω² = ½ × 0.008 × 314²
KE = 395 J stored energy

From high-speed video, metal-on-metal contact lasts about 10ms. Roughly half the stored energy transfers per hit—the rest goes to weapon rebound and opponent deformation.

Peak Impact Force F·Δt = ΔKE
F = 200 J / 0.01 s
F ≈ 20 kN peak impact

Design target: 3× FOS → 60 kN capacity

Bearing press-fits used H7/h6 (light interference, 0.01-0.02mm). The weapon shaft hex hub sees bearing stress of σ = F/A = 8.3 kN / 96 mm² = 87 MPa against 4140 steel at 415 MPa yield—that's an FOS of 4.8. Wheel guard mounting uses clearance fit H8/f7 so we can swap guards fast during competition.

Milled the backplate in-house on a manual Bridgeport. Ran 1000 RPM with a 6mm 2-flute HSS end mill (went HSS instead of carbide because carbide chips on interrupted cuts), 100 mm/min feed, 0.5mm depth of cut. Surface finish came out around Ra 3.2μm—not pretty, but it's structural, not cosmetic.

Wheel guards were laser cut on a Trotec Speedy 300—80W power, 10 mm/s (had to go slow to avoid melting and burrs on the HDPE). Kerf was about 0.2mm, wider than acrylic because HDPE expands more under heat.

Results

Fought 8 matches, took 2nd place. Replaced 3 wheel guards over the tournament (they're designed to be sacrificial—worked exactly as intended). One fork got bent during a particularly nasty hit, swapped it in about 10 minutes between rounds. Zero critical structural failures—backplate, weapon shaft, and bearings all survived the whole event. The modular, replaceable component strategy was the right call.

RC Car Drivetrain

Design & Fabrication Engineer — Drivetrain

8-week project · Gear failure → belt redesign

Final design with timing belt drivetrain

Initial spur gear design — teeth stripped after ~20 minutes

FEA — 12 MPa max stress at bolt hole, FOS = 4.2

Timing belt and pulley detail — HTD 5mm pitch

Overview

This one's a story about failure and fixing it. Started with 3D-printed PLA spur gears—two-stage reduction, 6.4:1 total ratio. Worked great for about 15-20 minutes, then the teeth stripped. Root cause: backlash in the gear mesh caused impact loading every time the drivetrain went from coast to drive. PLA's fatigue strength is only about 30 MPa at 10⁶ cycles, and the dynamic loading pushed tooth stress to around 50 MPa. So I scrapped the gears and redesigned around timing belts.

The Redesign

Timing belts solved every problem the gears had. HTD profile, 5mm pitch—zero backlash because the teeth interlock positively, plus the rubber composite absorbs transient loads and damps vibration. Kept the same 6.4:1 ratio (18T→36T first stage, 25T→80T second stage) so vehicle dynamics stayed the same. Ran 40+ minutes continuous without failure. Trade-offs: slightly lower efficiency (95% vs 98% per stage) and the belt stretches about 1mm per 10 hours, so the motor mount has slotted holes for tensioning.

Engineering Deep Dive

Steering Angles Wheelbase L = 200mm, Track T = 150mm
Min turn radius R = 300mm

θ_outer = tan⁻¹(L/(R+T)) = tan⁻¹(200/450) = 24.0°

Inner turns 9.7° more than outer → minimizes tire scrub

Ran ANSYS on the custom PLA pulley mount. Applied 50N belt tension (peak during acceleration—way more than the 11N steady-state from T/r) with PLA properties: E=3500 MPa, σ_yield=50 MPa.

Slider-Crank Mechanism

ME 350R/380R — Mechanism Design & Fabrication

Score: 54/50 · +4 bonus for exceeding specs

Final assembled mechanism

CAD model with link dimensions

Laser cutting test — dialing in kerf compensation

Press-fit calibration — 6.0mm, 5.9mm, 5.85mm iterations

Overview

Build a Grashof mechanism with less than 2mm joint play, less than 3mm out-of-plane motion, and carry a 10N load over a 50mm stroke. The mechanism had to rotate continuously 360°, which meant verifying the Grashof criterion before anything else. Laser-cut the acrylic links on a Trotec Speedy 300, 3D-printed the standoffs and bearing holders in PETG on a Bambu P1P.

The Press-Fit Story

This was the make-or-break challenge. The rotary bearings are 6mm ID, and achieving a tight press-fit in laser-cut acrylic without cracking it is finicky. First attempt: 6.0mm holes, 0.3mm play—bearing just rattled around. Second try: 5.9mm, got it down to 0.1mm play. Good but not great. Third try: 5.85mm holes gave less than 0.05mm play. Assembly needed a light mallet tap (maybe 50N of controlled force) but the fit was solid without cracking. That 0.05mm difference between "loose" and "perfect" is why I burned through three test cuts before committing to final parts.

Engineering Deep Dive

Link Lengths & Verification L1 = 100mm (ground), L2 = 60mm (crank)
L3 = 120mm (coupler), L4 = 80mm (slider)

Grashof: s + l ≤ p + q
60 + 120 = 180 ≤ 100 + 80 = 180
Satisfied — crank rotates full 360°

The 10N load test uses a 100mm unsupported cantilever—had to make sure the 3mm acrylic wouldn't snap or deflect enough to bind the mechanism.

Bending Stress M = F·L = 10N × 0.1m = 1 N·m
S = bh²/6 = (20 × 3²)/6 = 30 mm³
σ = M/S = 1000/30 = 33.3 MPa
Acrylic yield ≈ 70 MPa
FOS = 2.1 ✓
Deflection I = bh³/12 = (20 × 3³)/12 = 45 mm⁴
δ = FL³/(3EI) = (10 × 100³)/(3 × 3000 × 45)

Arduino Robot Car

ME 350R/380R — Mechatronics & Electronics Integration

Score: 52/50 · +2 aesthetics bonus

Top view — all wires within 2mm clearance

CAD layout with routing channels

Full assembly — acrylic chassis, PETG motor mounts

Overview

Joystick-controlled robot that had to carry a 0.5kg payload with professional cable management. The aesthetic bonus was basically "make it not look like a rats nest"—so I went all-in on wire routing. Designed channels directly into the laser-cut base plate, printed clip features into the motor mounts, and soldered every single connection (no breadboards, no loose jumper wires). The result: every wire is within 2mm of the chassis, zero protrusion beyond the footprint.

Engineering Deep Dive

Arduino Uno driving two DC gearmotors (6V, 200 RPM no-load, 48:1 gearbox) through an L298N dual H-bridge. The joystick outputs 0-1023 on two analog pins—I implemented a deadzone of ±50 counts around center (512) to prevent drift from noise, then mixed the X and Y axes for differential drive. Left motor gets Y + X/2, right motor gets Y − X/2, both constrained to the 0-255 PWM range.

Power Budget Motors at 50% load: 2 × 0.5A × 6V = 6W
Arduino: 50mA × 5V = 0.25W
L298N losses (~80% efficient): ~1.5W
Total: 7.75W → 1.55 hrs on 4×AA NiMH

Chassis is laser-cut 3mm acrylic base with 2mm top plate, 150×200mm. Motor mounts are 3D-printed PETG designed specifically for the 37mm diameter gearbox—4-point bolt pattern distributes load, and I integrated wire routing clips right into the mount geometry so there's nothing to snag during motion. The caster wheel has a height-adjustable mount (5mm range) for leveling.

Structural test: placed 0.5kg on the top plate, measured 0.3mm deflection at center with a dial indicator. No binding of internal components.

CNC Ball in Cube

CNC Machining & CAM Programming

Finished piece — hand-sanded to Ra ~0.4μm

Fusion360 CAM — adaptive clearing toolpath

After roughing — Ra ~3.2μm, visible tool marks

Overview

Started with a 76.2mm (3 inch) cube of 6061-T6 aluminum. The goal: machine a free-floating ball trapped inside a cube—can't be assembled, has to be cut from solid. That means 4 operations per face across 6 faces, with the ball never fully breaking free until the very last cut. The trickiest part is workholding: by face 5 you're clamping finished surfaces, so I printed PETG pads to protect the aluminum in the vise jaws.

Engineering Deep Dive

Roughing: 6mm 2-flute carbide flat end mill, 8000 RPM, 800 mm/min feed (0.05mm per tooth), 1.5mm stepover, 3mm depth of cut. Used adaptive clearing to keep tool engagement consistent and manage heat. Finishing: 6mm ball nose at 10,000 RPM, 600 mm/min, 0.2mm stepover—produces a theoretical scallop height that gets you to around Ra 1.6μm.

Cutting Force Check kc (6061-T6) ≈ 600 N/mm²
Fc = kc × ap × ae = 600 × 3 × 1.5 = 2700N
Spindle torque = 2700 × 0.003 = 8.1 N·m
Haas VF-2 max = 26.3 N·m at 8000 RPM
Utilization: 31% — no risk of stall

0.08mm (tol ±0.1mm)

Internal sphere came in at 47.9mm against a 48.0 target—0.1mm under, within the ±0.2mm tolerance. Ball clearance is about 1mm on each side, enough that it rolls freely without rattling too much. The final face was the scariest—left a 0.5mm "web" until all operations were done, then carefully broke through by hand.

Custom Graduation Gift

Design & Fabrication

Texas Inventionworks senior tradition

Top view — 5 material layers

Front elevation — layered construction with press-fit joints

Overview

At Texas Inventionworks, outgoing seniors get a custom-made gift from the team. It's a chance to combine personal meaning with fabrication skills—and the unwritten rule is no adhesives. Entire assembly has to be mechanical. I designed a five-layer piece: birch plywood base, then clear, white, and blue acrylic, topped with a 3D-printed PETG accent piece. Every layer connects through press-fit tabs.

Engineering Deep Dive

Getting the fit right meant accounting for laser kerf (0.15mm on the Trotec for acrylic, 0.12mm for wood). Tabs are 10mm wide, slots are cut at 9.7mm to give about 0.15mm interference after kerf. Assembly force is around 20N per joint—firm hand pressure, no cracking. Eight joints total gives 160N retention against maybe 50N needed to keep it together during normal handling. That's an FOS of 3.2.

Tolerance Stackup 5 layers × 3mm nominal = 15mm height
Kerf variation: ±0.02mm per cut
Material thickness: ±0.1mm (mfr spec)
Total: 15mm ± 0.5mm — acceptable for aesthetic piece

Performance across 4 environmental scenarios

Overview

The International Red Cross needed a way to keep people cool during extreme heat disasters—think 40°C desert days with 20+ occupants in a tent. My job was figuring out the thermal loads, sizing the AC system, and running CFD simulations in SolidWorks to validate it would actually work. The big question: can a 3-ton window AC unit keep the interior below 25°C when you've got solar radiation, 20 people generating body heat, and a tent that barely insulates?

Thermal Load Breakdown

I broke down every source of heat entering the tent. The numbers add up fast.

Engineering Deep Dive

Conduction Through Tent Envelope Tent area: 2(4×2.5) + 2(5×2.5) + 4×5 = 65 m²
R = 0.5 m²·K/W (reflective polyester)
ΔT = 40°C − 25°C = 15 K
Q_cond = 65 × 15 / 0.5 = 1,950 W
Solar Radiation (Peak) G = 1000 W/m² (clear sky midday)
Roof area = 20 m², α = 0.30 absorptance
Q_solar = 1000 × 20 × 0.30 = 6,000 W
Occupant Metabolic Heat 20 people × 100 W/person (ASHRAE sedentary)
Q_people = 2,000 W
Infiltration V = 50 m³, ACH = 3 (tent with flaps)
Q = ρ·Cp·V̇·ΔT = 1.2 × 1005 × (150/3600) × 15
Q_inf = 753 W

Set up the simulation in SolidWorks Flow Sim with a 250,000-cell tetrahedral mesh—refined down to 5cm near the AC vents and occupant zones, coarser at 20cm in the far field. Used k-ε turbulence model (standard for room airflow), 40°C external ambient with 2 m/s wind, AC supply air at 15°C and 400 CFM.

The hot-dry scenario (40°C, 20% RH) hit 24.5°C interior average—right on target. There's a 5°C stratification from floor to ceiling peak, which is expected. Air velocities in the occupied zone are 0.2-0.8 m/s, comfortable range. Heat flux through the walls matched my hand calcs almost exactly (30 W/m²), which was a nice sanity check.

The humid scenario (35°C, 80% RH) was marginal—26°C average, slightly above target. But the wet-bulb temperature came in at 23°C, which is still safe for prolonged exposure per OSHA guidelines. Added a latent load calculation for 20 people sweating: about 628W extra from moisture evaporation.

38,200 BTU/hr total load ÷ 12,000 BTU/ton = 3.2 tons minimum. Went with a 3-ton (36,000 BTU/hr) unit—6% undersized at absolute peak, but occupants can handle brief periods above 25°C and peak solar doesn't last all day.

Generator Sizing AC: 3,500W (COP ≈ 3.0)
Lighting + medical + fans: 600W
Total steady-state: 4,100W
+ 20% margin = 5 kW minimum

5kW diesel @ 75% load: 0.6 gal/hr
3.4 gal tank → 5.7 hr runtime
Daily fuel (12hr): 7.2 gallons

Also analyzed evaporative cooling (works great in dry heat at only 300W, but useless above 80% RH), passive ventilation (would need 112°C interior to remove the heat—obviously not viable), and solar+battery ($9,500 vs $3,000 for diesel, but eliminates fuel logistics for permanent installations).

Validated Scenarios

Desert Day (40°C, 20% RH)
24.5°C interior ✓
Humid Day (35°C, 80% RH)
26°C / WB 23°C ✓
Overcast (38°C, 40% RH)
23°C interior ✓
Night (25°C)
22-24°C, 60% power ✓

Final system: 3-ton window AC + 5kW diesel generator, ~$7,000 per station. Capacity for 20 people lying down or 30 seated. Setup time around 2-3 hours with four people.
