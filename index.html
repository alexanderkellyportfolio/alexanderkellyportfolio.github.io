SXSW BattleBot IQ

Hardware Engineer, Structural Design & Manufacturing

5 weeks · 15-lb class · 2nd place at SXSW

SolidWorksManual MillingLaser CuttingMaterial SelectionDFM/DFATolerance AnalysisImpact Load Analysis

Context

UT's robotics department needed a 15-lb combat robot for SXSW with about five weeks of lead time. My responsibility was the structural design: backplate, wheel guards, modular forks, and the mounting scheme that tied everything together. The whole design was built around field repairability since we knew components would take damage during competition.

Full assembly, top view

Material Selection

The backplate is 6061-T6 aluminum. Yield strength of 275 MPa at 2.7 g/cm3 gives a strength-to-weight ratio around 102 MPa per unit density. I looked at 7075-T6 as well (stronger at 570 MPa but too brittle under impact loading, and you can't weld it if something cracks in the field) and 2024 (aerospace-grade, more expensive than we needed). 6061 was the clear choice for a combat application where machinability and repairability matter.

Wheel guards are HDPE at Shore D 66 hardness. Low friction means opponents slide off on contact, and the material absorbs impacts elastically without cracking. I tested 3mm thickness first but it cracked, then 6mm which held up, and 8mm was too heavy. The weapon drum is 4340 hardened steel, heat treated to 470 MPa yield.

SolidWorks CAD model

Manufacturing

I milled the backplate on a manual Bridgeport at 1000 RPM with a 6mm 2-flute HSS end mill, 100 mm/min feed rate, 0.5mm depth of cut. Went with HSS over carbide because carbide tends to chip on interrupted cuts. Surface finish was around Ra 3.2 microns, which is fine for a structural part.

Milling the backplate, 1000 RPM, 100mm/min feed

Wheel guards were laser cut on a Trotec Speedy 300 at 80W, 10 mm/s. Had to keep the speed low to avoid melting the HDPE.

Engineering Details

The drum weapon stores a meaningful amount of kinetic energy. I measured the moment of inertia using a torsion pendulum (I = 0.008 kg m2) and the belt system caps out around 3000 RPM.

Stored Kinetic Energyw = 3000 x 2pi/60 = 314 rad/s
KE = 0.5 x I x w^2 = 0.5 x 0.008 x 314^2
KE = 395 J

From high-speed video, metal-on-metal contact is about 10ms. Roughly half the energy transfers per hit.

Peak Impact ForceF = 200 J / 0.01 s
F = 20 kN peak

Design target: 3x FOS = 60 kN structural capacity

Bearing press-fits used H7/h6 tolerance (light interference, 0.01-0.02mm). The weapon shaft hex hub sees a bearing stress of 87 MPa on 4140 steel at 415 MPa yield, so FOS of 4.8. Wheel guard mounting uses clearance fit H8/f7 for quick replacement during competition.

After 8 matches

8 matches, 2nd place finish. I replaced 3 wheel guards over the course of the tournament, which was expected since they're designed as sacrificial armor. One fork got bent and I swapped it in about 10 minutes between rounds. The backplate, weapon shaft, and all bearings survived the entire event without failure.

RC Car Drivetrain

Design & Fabrication Engineer, Drivetrain

8-week academic project

OnshapeANSYS FEADrivetrain DesignFailure AnalysisBelt DrivesShaft SizingBearing SelectionTolerance Analysis

Context

Class project where we built an RC car from scratch. I was responsible for the rear-wheel-drive drivetrain and all the associated mounting. The car also had an Ackermann steering system on the front end. My initial spur gear design didn't hold up, so the project ended up being as much about diagnosing the failure and redesigning as it was about the original design work.

Initial design with spur gears

Gear Failure and Redesign

The first drivetrain used 3D-printed PLA spur gears in a two-stage reduction (10T to 20T, then 15T to 48T, total 6.4:1). After about 15-20 minutes of running, the teeth stripped. The root cause was backlash in the gear mesh creating impact loads on every drive-coast-drive transition. PLA fatigue strength is only around 30 MPa at 10^6 cycles, and the dynamic loading was pushing tooth stress to roughly 50 MPa.

Redesigned with timing belt system

The timing belt redesign used HTD profile belts with 5mm pitch. First stage: 18T motor pulley to 36T jackshaft (2:1). Second stage: 25T jackshaft to 80T axle (3.2:1). Same 6.4:1 total ratio so vehicle dynamics stayed the same. Key improvements: zero backlash from positive tooth engagement, elastic absorption of transient loads (about 40% peak stress reduction), and vibration damping. Ran 40+ minutes without issues.

HTD timing belt and pulley assembly

Engineering Details

Ran ANSYS on the PLA pulley mount with 50N belt tension (peak during acceleration) and PLA material properties (E=3500 MPa, yield=50 MPa).

Slider-Crank Mechanism

ME 350R, Mechanism Design & Fabrication

Laser cutting · 3D printing · Press-fit iteration

Laser Cutting3D Printing (PETG)Mechanism DesignGrashof AnalysisBeam BendingPress-Fit DesignTolerance Iteration

Overview

The assignment was to build a Grashof mechanism that could rotate continuously with less than 2mm of joint play, less than 3mm out-of-plane motion, and support a 10N load over a 50mm stroke. I laser-cut the acrylic linkages on the Trotec Speedy 300 and 3D-printed the standoffs, bearing holders, and feet in PETG on a Bambu P1P.

CAD model with link dimensions

Press-Fit Iteration

This was the hardest part of the project. The rotary bearings are 6mm ID and getting a tight press-fit in laser-cut acrylic without cracking it requires some iteration. My first test cut used 6.0mm holes, which left about 0.3mm of play. The bearing just rattled in there. Second attempt at 5.9mm brought it down to 0.1mm, which was better but still not where I wanted it. Third round at 5.85mm gave less than 0.05mm play. Assembly needed a light tap with a mallet (maybe 50N of force) but the fit was solid and nothing cracked.

Press-fit test cuts: 6.0mm, 5.9mm, 5.85mm

Laser cut test pieces, dialing in kerf compensation

Arduino Robot Car

ME 350R, Mechatronics & Electronics Integration

Differential drive · All-soldered connections

Arduino (C/C++)SolderingLaser Cutting3D Printing (PETG)Circuit DesignPWM Motor ControlPower Budgeting

Overview

The assignment was a joystick-controlled robot that could carry a 0.5kg payload. There was a separate grading criterion for cable management, so I designed the wire routing into the laser-cut chassis from the start rather than trying to clean it up after the fact. I cut channels directly into the acrylic base plate and integrated clip features into the 3D-printed motor mounts.

Top view, all wires within 2mm clearance

Electronics & Controls

The control system is an Arduino Uno driving two DC gearmotors (6V, 200 RPM no-load, 48:1 gearbox) through an L298N dual H-bridge. The joystick outputs 0-1023 on the analog pins. I set up a deadzone of ±50 counts around center to prevent motor drift from noise, then mixed X and Y axes for differential steering. Left motor gets the Y value plus half of X, right motor gets Y minus half of X, both constrained to the PWM range.

CAD layout with routing channels and mount locations

Power DrawMotors at 50% load: 2 × 0.5A × 6V = 6W
Arduino: 50mA × 5V = 0.25W
L298N losses (~80% eff): ~1.5W
Total: 7.75W → 1.55 hrs on 4×AA NiMH

Mechanical Design

Chassis is a 150×200mm laser-cut acrylic base (3mm) with a 2mm top plate. Motor mounts are PETG printed to match the 37mm gearbox diameter with a 4-point bolt pattern. The caster wheel mount has 5mm of height adjustment for leveling. For the structural test, I placed 0.5kg on the top plate and measured 0.3mm deflection at center with a dial indicator.

CNC Ball in Cube

CNC Machining & CAM Programming

3 weeks · 6 setups · ~4 hours machine time

Fusion360 CAM3-Axis CNCMulti-Setup WorkholdingToolpath OptimizationFeeds & SpeedsSurface FinishGD&T

Overview

The goal was to machine a free-floating ball trapped inside a cube from a solid block of 6061-T6 aluminum (76.2mm / 3 inch cube). It can't be assembled after the fact, so everything has to be cut from solid stock. That means 4 operations per face across 6 faces, and the ball can't break free until the very last cut on the very last face.

Fusion360 CAM, adaptive clearing toolpath

Process

Each face gets the same sequence: roughing with a 6mm flat end mill for bulk removal, spiral finishing with a 6mm ball nose for the internal sphere surface, edge finishing, and a 0.5mm × 45° chamfer. The trickiest part is workholding. By face 5 you're clamping on finished surfaces, so I printed PETG protective pads for the vise jaws. For the final face, I left a 0.5mm web intact until all operations were done, then carefully broke through by hand.

After roughing, Ra ~3.2μm

5 of 6 faces done

Roughing: 8000 RPM, 800 mm/min feed (0.05mm/tooth), 1.5mm stepover, 3mm depth of cut. Finishing: 10,000 RPM, 600 mm/min, 0.2mm stepover for about Ra 1.6μm theoretical.

Overview

At Texas Inventionworks, outgoing seniors get a custom gift built by the team. The rule is no adhesives, so the whole thing has to hold together mechanically. I designed a five-layer piece: birch plywood base, then clear, white, and blue acrylic, topped with a PETG accent piece. Everything connects through press-fit tabs.

Top view, 5 material layers

Press-Fit Design

The fit had to account for laser kerf (0.15mm for acrylic on the Trotec, 0.12mm for wood). Tabs are 10mm wide, slots are cut at 9.7mm to give about 0.15mm interference after kerf. Each joint takes about 20N of hand pressure to assemble. With 8 joints total that's 160N retention force against the roughly 50N needed to keep it together during handling, so about a 3.2 FOS.

Red Cross Cooling Station

Thermal Analysis Lead, ME 266K Senior Design

Full semester · IFRC client · CFD & thermal analysis

SolidWorks Flow SimCFDHeat TransferHVAC SizingPsychrometricsTurbulence ModelingPower Budgeting

Problem

The International Red Cross needed a rapidly deployable cooling solution for extreme heat events. Think 40°C desert day, 20+ people in a tent, minimal infrastructure. My job was to figure out the thermal loads, size the AC system, and run CFD in SolidWorks to validate that the design would actually keep people below safe temperature thresholds.

Thermal Load Breakdown

I calculated every heat source entering the tent envelope individually. The numbers add up fast.

CFD Validation

I set up the simulation in SolidWorks Flow Sim with a 250,000 cell tetrahedral mesh, refined to 5cm near the AC vents and occupant zones. Used the k-ε turbulence model which is standard for indoor airflow applications. Boundary conditions were 40°C ambient with 2 m/s wind, AC supply at 15°C and 400 CFM.

The hot-dry scenario (40°C, 20% RH) came out to 24.5°C average interior temperature, right on target. There's a 5°C stratification from floor to ceiling which is expected. The heat flux through the walls matched my hand calcs almost exactly at 30 W/m², which was a good sanity check on the simulation setup.

The humid scenario (35°C, 80% RH) was more marginal at 26°C average, slightly above the 25°C target. But the wet-bulb temperature came in at 23°C, still safe for prolonged exposure per OSHA guidelines. I added a latent load calculation for 20 people sweating which contributes about 628W of additional moisture-related load.

38,200 BTU/hr ÷ 12,000 BTU/ton = 3.2 tons minimum. Went with a 3-ton unit (36,000 BTU/hr), which is about 6% undersized at absolute peak but acceptable since occupants can tolerate brief excursions above 25°C and peak solar is only a few hours per day.

Generator SizingAC: 3,500W (COP ≈ 3.0)
Lighting + medical + fans: 600W
Total: 4,100W + 20% margin = 5 kW min

5kW diesel @ 75% load: 0.6 gal/hr
Daily fuel (12hr): 7.2 gal, $7k per station

I also analyzed evaporative cooling (effective in dry heat at only 300W draw but useless above 80% RH), passive ventilation (would need over 100°C interior to remove the heat, obviously not viable), and solar+battery ($9,500 vs $3,000 for diesel, better for permanent installations).
